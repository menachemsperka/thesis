Methods for Addressing Extreme Label Imbalance in Small NER Datasets. Named Entity Recognition (NER) is notably prone to extreme label imbalance. In many NER corpora, a single non-entity category (“O”) accounts for over 80% of tokens (Nemoto et al., 2025). Moreover, the most common named entity type can appear tens to hundreds of times more frequently than the least common type, and in specialized or low-resource datasets some rare entity types may even have fewer than ten total occurrences (Wang & Wang, 2022). This long-tailed distribution violates the usual assumption of identically distributed class frequencies across training and test sets and causes standard models to overfit the majority class while under-learning the rare classes. Recent surveys underscore that mitigating data imbalance—through redistribution and augmentation strategies—is now an essential part of NER pipelines in low-resource settings (Huang et al., 2025). 

To ensure that uncommon entity types receive adequate representation in training, the literature suggests a three-stage approach: (1) label-aware data splitting, (2) training-time reweighting or resampling, and (3) data augmentation for rare entities (Huang et al., 2025; Wang & Wang, 2022). We summarize each in turn, highlighting how they bolster rare-label representation and noting key methods from major studies. 

1. Label-Aware Data Splitting. The first step is to partition the data so that all entity types — especially the rare ones — appear in each subset (training, validation, test) if possible. Random splits can accidentally exclude infrequent labels from the training or test set, which is irreparable later (Sechidis et al., 2011). One solution is iterative stratification, a label-aware greedy selection method originally developed for multi-label classification (Sechidis et al., 2011). Iterative stratification treats each sentence as a multi-label instance (with labels corresponding to its entity types) and repeatedly assigns the sentence containing the current least-represented label to the subset that most needs that label. By prioritizing rare labels during split construction, this strategy yields a more balanced distribution of labels across splits and sharply reduces “zero-positive” cases where an entity type is missing from a given fold. 

More advanced splitting techniques use constrained optimization to achieve near min–max balanced label distributions. For example, EvoSplit (Florez-Revuelta, 2021) is an evolutionary algorithm that optimizes label frequencies in each fold while respecting the desired subset sizes. The outcome is that each subset has an approximately equal representation of each entity type, without skewing the overall train/test ratio (Florez-Revuelta, 2021). Such label-aware splits are highly evaluation-protective, aiming for evaluation-guaranteed scenarios where no label is absent in any subset. Even if a perfect guarantee is not always feasible, studies show that these methods vastly decrease the chance of missing rare labels in any fold (Sechidis et al., 2011; Florez-Revuelta, 2021). In summary, careful splitting prevents rare classes from being neglected at the outset, which is a prerequisite for effective learning and fair evaluation on those classes. 

2. Training-Time Reweighting and Resampling. The second stage is to adjust how the learning algorithm treats the imbalanced training data. Inverse-frequency weighting is a straightforward method: assign higher loss weights to errors on rare classes so the model is “incentivized” to learn from sparse examples (Nemoto et al., 2025). In token-level sequence labeling, this typically means weighting each token’s loss by the inverse of its label’s frequency. However, direct inverse weighting can be brittle or cause over-correction in extreme imbalance (Nemoto et al., 2025). Researchers have therefore proposed log-scaled or sublinear weighting to moderate the influence of very rare classes (Nemoto et al., 2025). Another complementary approach is rare-label boosted sampling during training: oversample sentences that contain infrequent entity types (and/or undersample those without them) so that rare-class instances appear more often in each epoch. Wang and Wang (2022) showed that a carefully designed sentence-level resampling scheme—using a rarity score based on inverse label frequency with smoothing—can yield large gains in low-resource NER performance, particularly by improving recall for minority entity categories. These adjustments ensure that rare entities contribute proportionally more to the model’s training, counteracting the overwhelming prevalence of the “O” class and other very common entities. 

3. Data Augmentation for Rare Entities. The third intervention is to generate synthetic training instances to boost the presence of rare entities. Modern techniques use pre-trained models to produce new labeled examples for minority classes. For instance, masked language model augmentation like MELM (Masked Entity Language Modeling) leverages BERT-style models to replace an existing entity mention with a different plausible mention of the same type (Zhou et al., 2022). By masking an entity token or span and conditioning on its label type and context, MELM creates a new sentence containing a rare-type entity in a natural linguistic context. This label-conditioned augmentation yields additional training examples for the rarest classes and has been shown to significantly improve their recognition (Zhou et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2025). 

Other methods aim to increase rare entities through simpler, controlled transformations. One technique is context-preserving duplication: duplicating or lightly rephrasing sentences that contain rare labels so they appear more frequently in training (Dai & Adel, 2020). This can include synonym substitution for non-entity words or same-type entity replacement (e.g., swapping one location name for another), which adds data diversity without losing the original label or context (Dai & Adel, 2020). Such conservative augmentation has proven useful in low-resource NER, whereas aggressive synthetic generation may introduce noise and degrade performance (Wang & Wang, 2022; Archana & Prakash, 2024). To avoid over-augmentation, researchers often apply budget-based generation: limiting the number of new sentences per original and focusing generation on the most underrepresented entity types (Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). This kind of label-aware augmentation schedule ensures that the worst-off classes get needed examples while preventing the data distribution from being overly distorted. 

Proposed Contribution. This thesis builds on these strategies by introducing a unified approach to extreme NER imbalance. We compare three sentence-level splitting strategies—a simple random baseline, a label-aware greedy split, and multilabel stratified splitting—to quantify how label-aware data partitioning affects rare-entity recognition. Additionally, we develop a label-aware augmentation scheme that dynamically allocates a limited augmentation budget to the most underrepresented entity types. These contributions extend prior work by systematically evaluating splitting strategies alongside targeted augmentation, jointly aiming to maximize rare-label representation throughout the NER training pipeline.
